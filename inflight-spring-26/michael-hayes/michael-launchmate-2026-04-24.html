Founder Reflection — Week 17

What Building Auth Taught Me About Product

I spent three weeks on something I kept trying to make simpler. The lesson wasn't about auth at all.

For Craig — the product story behind the MCP server, not the implementation.

scroll ↓
What I was trying to do

Let the tools talk to each other

The connector pivot means founders don't type updates into LaunchMate — their tools push context automatically. But for tools to push context, they need to prove who they are. That's auth.

Seemed straightforward. I already had a login system (Clerk handles Google OAuth for the web app). Just... let MCP clients use the same thing, right?

Not right. The login system I built for humans doesn't work for machines. The web app uses browser cookies and sessions. But an MCP client like Cursor — running on a founder's laptop — needs something different. It can't store cookies. It can't keep a browser session. It needs a token it can hand over on every request.

That mismatch is where the three weeks started.

I kept building code to bridge the gap between "how my app logs people in" and "how external tools prove who they are" — without ever stopping to ask if those two things should be solved the same way.
2/4
The pattern I kept repeating

Build, hit a wall, rebuild

I went through six different approaches to auth. Each one felt right until I tested it against a real client and discovered something it couldn't handle.

The cycle
  • Build an auth approach → "This feels clean"
  • Test against a real client → "Oh, Cursor can't do that"
  • Scrap it, try something else → "Maybe if I simplify..."
  • Repeat

The worst part: at one point I tried to skip inbound MCP entirely — just make LaunchMate call other tools, never receive calls. That would have abandoned the connector thesis. I was literally proposing to kill the product strategy because auth was hard.

Then I found what felt like the escape hatch: just validate Google tokens. We already use Google OAuth. MCP clients already have Google tokens. Just check the token, look up the user, done. No custom auth system needed.

And it worked. For one specific client. The one that runs on our servers.

The simplification wasn't wrong. It was incomplete. I'd solved for one client and assumed the others didn't matter.
3/4
What I actually learned

Name your clients before you build

LaunchMate's MCP has three real clients. They look similar but they're not the same:

Cursor & Claude

Running on a founder's laptop. Can't store secrets safely. Needs to redirect through a browser to log the human in. Needs a way to register itself automatically.

Poke

Running on our servers. CAN store secrets safely. Just needs to prove "I'm Poke, I'm allowed to push transcripts." Much simpler.

Once I named the actual clients and their actual constraints, the auth design was obvious. Cursor and Claude need the full OAuth flow — dynamic registration, browser redirects, the works. Poke just needs a client ID and secret. Both paths hit the same server. Two client types, one auth system.

Three rules I'm taking away:

  • Name every client before writing auth code. "External tools" is too vague. "Cursor, Claude, and Poke" is buildable. Each has different constraints that change the design.
  • "Simpler" isn't the same as "sufficient." The Google-only approach was simpler but only worked for one of three clients. The right architecture handles all of them — and it's only more complex in the places it needs to be.
  • Don't escape hard problems — scope them. I kept trying to avoid the full OAuth flow instead of asking: which clients actually need it, and what exactly do they need from it? The answer was smaller than I feared.
The auth was never the problem. The problem was building before I understood what I was building for. Once I could name the clients and their constraints, the design emerged. Three weeks of pivoting compressed into the question I should have asked on day one: "Who are the clients, and what does each one need?"
