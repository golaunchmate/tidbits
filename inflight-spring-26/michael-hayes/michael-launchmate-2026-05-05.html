Connectors Are Permission Design

Three connectors went live. The interesting part isn't that they work — it's that each one asks for a different amount of trust.

When "connect your tools" becomes "how much of your work can I see?"

What shipped

Three connectors, three trust levels

LaunchMate now has connectors in account settings. You sign in, you connect your tools, and the agent gets context. That's the theory. In practice, each connector asks for a different kind of permission — and that's where the product design lives.

Granola — meeting transcripts
Self-discoverable MCP. Sign in via OAuth. Done.
High trust
Notion — workspace docs
Create an auth client. Configure what to extract. Optional: collaborator emails.
Medium trust
Google Drive — all your files
Create an auth client. Grants access to your entire Drive. No scoping yet.
Low trust

The trust gradient isn't a bug. It's a map of where the product needs to grow.

2/4
The tension

Speed now, trust later

The Google Drive connector is the clearest example of the tradeoff. Two options:

  • Full Drive access — ships today. Every file visible. Maximum context for the agent. But the founder sees "LaunchMate wants to read everything in your Google Drive" and hesitates.
  • Folder-scoped access — better long-term. Founder creates a "LaunchMate" folder, puts relevant files there. Permission feels bounded. But it's more setup, less context, and it's not built yet.

I chose full Drive access for now. The reasoning: ship the connector, validate it works, then tighten the permissions. Speed over trust in the short term. Trust over speed in the long term.

But here's what I noticed: the moment I said "full Drive access," I felt the discomfort myself. I wasn't just making an implementation choice — I was making a trust decision that affects whether someone will actually connect the tool.

Notion already shows the better path. You can choose what gets extracted. You can opt out of collaborator emails. Granular permissions aren't a feature — they're the difference between "this is useful" and "this sees too much."

3/4
What this means

Permission is the product

The connector pivot said: "Founders shouldn't type updates. Their tools should push context automatically." That's the architecture. But the connector surface says something different:

"How much of your work are you willing to let me see?"

That question is the product. Not the pipeline. Not the OAuth flow. The moment where the founder decides how much trust to extend.

Three things I'm taking from this:

  • Every permission request is a trust checkpoint. Granola feels easy because meeting transcripts are expected to be shared. Google Drive feels hard because "my files" feels personal.
  • Broad access ships faster. Scoped access retains users. The short-term/long-term tradeoff is real. The question is when to switch from one to the other.
  • The discomfort is data. When I felt weird about full Drive access, that wasn't a weakness in my reasoning. That was a signal about how founders will feel.

Folder-scoped Drive access is the next trust upgrade. Not because it's technically better, but because it's the permission model that matches what founders actually want: helpful context from specific places, not surveillance of everything.
