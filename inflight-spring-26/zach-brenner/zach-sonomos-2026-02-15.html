1/5

Where I Started

I thought I was selling AI monitoring software to law firms.

The plan was straightforward: reach out to managing partners, show them the detection tech, close the sale.

I knew there'd be organizational friction — no one owns AI policy, firms don't want to look under the hood, "Schrödinger's Compliance."

But I figured if the tech was good enough, that would overcome it.

2/5

What I Discovered

Three signals changed the picture entirely:

1. Swiss Re reached out to me

Not the other way around. They want a phone call and product demo. That's a PULL signal — insurance carriers might BE the customer, not just a channel.

2. Law firms buy through consultants

SDSU entrepreneurship prof: professional services firms are too busy for cold outreach. They buy through trusted IT/cybersecurity consultants who already have relationships.

3. My perfectionism is the blocker

I said: "The tool isn't ready to distribute."
Neal said: "It's ready. You'll evolve it over time."

3/5

The Real Opportunity

Neal's Reframe

"Your innovation is not the detection — it's the distribution hack."

Detection code? Easily replicated by competitors.

Browser extension in attorneys' workflows? Hard to displace.

  • Real defensibility: Getting real estate in the browser
  • Emotional hook: Guilt about free AI use drives adoption
  • Moat = adoption, not algorithm

This reframes everything about what I'm building and who I'm building for.

4/5
