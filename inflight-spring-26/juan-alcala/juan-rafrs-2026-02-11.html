1/5

Where I Started

Welders said "that's fucking sick."

I brought our carbon fiber repair kit to Workshops for Warriors — a welding school full of veterans and retired Navy guys. The reaction was better than I expected.

The big moment: I showed them a galvanized steel tube bonded to a thin aluminum plate. The welder looked at it and said:

"For me to weld steel, it has to be really hot... that's going to be too hot for aluminum, there's going to be a hole through this whole thing. You otherwise can't put those two things together."

→ Dissimilar metal joining — confirmed impossible with traditional welding

→ Referrals to senior instructors, including a retired Navy welder with 20+ years

→ Demo with them scheduled for Wednesday

Felt great. Problem validated. Tech validated. But then my mentors asked a harder question.

2/5

What I Discovered

"You're a hammer looking for a nail."

Neal and Craig sat me down. The feedback was direct:

→ The tech is cool. But nobody's gripping the table wanting it.

→ Welders are impressed — but they're not the buyer.

→ Craig: "Don't frame this as welding. You'll never be as good as welding."

Then a positioning analysis landed that reframed everything:

The Insight

The real barrier to DoD adoption isn't technical — it's political.

Approvers have asymmetric risk: if they approve RAFRS and it fails, they're blamed. If they reject it and equipment stays broken, nobody notices.

They don't need more test data. They need someone else to go first.

The Real Opportunity

Stop competing. Create the category.

The framing that wins:

✗ "Better than tape" → loses to Flex Tape on price

✗ "Alternative to welding" → invites a debate I can't win yet

✓ "Field repair when welding can't show up" → no competition. Just broken equipment.

The 10-Day Sprint

Neal gave me four tests. Come back Feb 20 with data, not opinions:

→ A/B Landing Pages: "Carbon fiber repair tape" vs. "Cold welding — no heat, no electricity"

→ Boat launch / marina: Find the guy whose trailer bracket just cracked

→ El Segundo manufacturing hub: Knock on doors, show the kit

→ Machine shop hang-out: Sit there all day. Listen for repair problems.

The question isn't "does it work?" anymore. It's "who's trying to solve this problem today and failing?"
