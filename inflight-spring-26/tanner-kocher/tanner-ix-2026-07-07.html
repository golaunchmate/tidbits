Where I Started

I thought I needed to explain why traceability matters

For months, I'd been building compliance infrastructure for AI-generated decisions in clinical trials. The pitch was always the same: "AI is generating decisions in regulated environments, and nobody can trace where they came from."

Most people nodded politely. Some got it. But the market wasn't moving fast enough.

Meanwhile, 17+ companies launched agent memory infrastructure in June-July. Couchbase. Microsoft Memora. AWS AgentCore. Databricks. Mandol. Elastic Atlas. All horizontal. None targeting clinical trials.

I was watching the infrastructure layer commoditize in real time — and wondering if I'd picked the wrong fight.

On July 6, the FDA issued its first AI-related CGMP warning letter — Purolea Cosmetics used AI to draft drug specs without review. The FDA's message: "AI can assist but cannot replace the firm's responsibility to perform required GMP activities."

Same day: FDA adopted ICH E6(R3) — data governance elevated to a standalone section. Sponsors AND investigators now responsible for data integrity, audit trails, and computerized systems validation. 66% of sites expect increased burden.

Earlier that week: WCG report showed only 11% of pharma firms have fully implemented AI in clinical trials. 89% haven't. "Enthusiasm without governance infrastructure is now a liability."

The FDA didn't just validate my thesis. It codified it into regulation.

The Real Opportunity

The infrastructure is commoditizing. The moat is the regulation.

Everyone is building agent memory. Microsoft Memora gets 98% token reduction. Mandol does 5.4x faster recall. Couchbase has enterprise distribution. The infrastructure layer is becoming a crowded, horizontal market.

But here's what none of them have:

  • Deterministic linking — zero LLM inference for edge creation
  • Spectral structural calibration — graph topology, not just vector similarity
  • MVCC versioning — immutable audit trail, time-travel foundations
  • HIPAA-aligned architecture — designed for clinical trials from day one
  • FDA-compliant traceability — now validated by a warning letter

The FDA isn't asking for "agent memory." It's asking for decision traceability — who made this decision, when, based on what evidence, reviewed by whom, stored how, retrievable for how long.

That's not a feature. That's the entire product. And Ix is the only company building it for clinical trials.
