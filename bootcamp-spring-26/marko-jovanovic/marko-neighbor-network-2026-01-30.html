From broken elevators to tenant power

1/5

Where I Started

Interview with Nick

We talked to Nick, a kinesiology senior living on the 5th floor of off-campus housing. Both elevators have been broken since December.

He's walking 5-7 flights of stairs daily. Management sends AI-generated non-answers. A lawyer told him there's no legal recourse — the elevator isn't even in his lease.

"They just see us as money. They don't see us as real people."
2/5

What I Discovered

The community power is there

Nick and his roommate went door to door. People opened up. They felt united. Multiple households had already complained separately.

The problem? All that work accumulated to nothing.

"How do we take that community aspect and put that force towards making a message?"

The insight: Tenants have collective power but no mechanism to use it.

3/5

The Real Opportunity

Neighbor Network

Make organizing your building as easy as a group chat.

  • Join by address
  • See active issues in your building
  • Add your name to collective action
  • Coordinate without knocking on every door

Why this direction: Door-to-door worked — it just doesn't scale. Nick proved tenants will engage. We need to make that engagement persist.

4/5

Where I'm At

Building the prototype

Next step: Create a simple mockup showing what a building tenant network looks like — then get back to Nick.

Validation question: "If this existed in December, would it have made organizing easier?"

"I thought the problem was broken elevators.
It's actually broken leverage."

5/5
